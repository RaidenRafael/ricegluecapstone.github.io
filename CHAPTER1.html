CHAPTER I

INTRODUCTION

A glue or an adhesive can be any non-metallic material that is applied to one or both surfaces of two distinct objects to bind them together and prevent them from separating; it is also referred to as glue, cement, mucilage, or paste (Athavale, 2018). This implies that glue has been around for centuries according to Bellis (2019). Clay pots that had been mended with adhesive created from tree sap have been found by archaeologists excavating 4000 BC burial sites. The Greeks are known to have made adhesives for use in carpentry and to incorporate components for their glue formulations, including things like egg whites, blood, bones, milk, cheese, vegetables, and grains. The Romans employed tar and beeswax as glue. For this reason, the researchers decided to use rice, a sticky material, to attempt to produce such glue.

Rice is one of the essentials of the human diet and one of the most commonly grown cereal crops. Throughout history, rice has most likely nourished more people than any other crop (Rosell & Marco, 2008). The two kinds of starch found in rice grains, amylose, and amylopectin, determine how well they cook. The amount of amylose starch found in the two varieties of rice varies. Non-glutinous rice, like basmati, has more amylose than amylopectin, but glutinous rice has more amylopectin than amylose (Mookerjee, 2023). Knowing this, the researchers decided to take advantage of the amylopectin, the type of starch that makes rice stick, to produce an adhesive.

A rice glue also known as sokui (続飯), is a gel or liquid adhesive which can be created by smoothing well-cooked white rice and diluting it to the ideal thickness with water. It has been used for many kinds of arts and crafts since ancient times, including paper and woodworking (Rice Glue, 2022). Analyzing the Effectiveness of Leftover Rice into glue, can produce a safer and less harmful adhesive suitable for a range of applications such as arts and crafts. Additionally, the researchers decided to produce Rice Glue aimed for students to use in their academic activities to reduce waste and produce glue that won’t negatively affect the students’ health.

It also considers the eco-friendliness of using leftover rice, focusing on aspects like biodegradability, safety, and sustainability. This project also has limitations which are the adhesive strength of rice starch, which may not match the performance of commercial synthetic glues, particularly for applications requiring strong and durable bonds. Additionally, the compatibility of rice starch adhesive with different materials could be variable, potentially limiting its effectiveness across various substrates. Another significant factor to consider is the susceptibility of rice starch adhesive to moisture, which could impact its effectiveness and durability when used in humid conditions or when exposed to water.

The adhesive properties of rice glue might not provide strong adhesion on hard surfaces such as wood or other materials, thus restricting its use in such situations. Although it performs well in bonding paper-based items, its efficacy decreases on surfaces with uneven textures or hardness, thereby limiting its usefulness for projects or activities involving non-smooth items.

CHAPTER III

METHODOLOGY

        This research methodology provides an approach for assessing the efficacy of transforming leftover rice into glue, focusing on the analysis of glue characteristics.

Materials

  • 1 Blender                                                                                            
  • 1 Strainer
  • Measuring Cup
  • Oil
  • 1 Small pot
  • Vinegar
  • Baking soda
  • Leftover Rice
  • Bowl
  • 1 Tablespoon

Procedures

Step 1:  Gather & prepare your leftover rice.

Step 2: Prepare a pot and ignite the stove to a low heat.

Step 3: Pour half a cup of water into the pot.

Step 4: Put the amount of rice you’ll turn to glue into the pot.

Step 5: Gently spread the rice in the pot until it's just about soaked in water, not submerged, then stir until its consistency is that of oatmeal. (Add more rice and water, and adjust the heat if necessary)

Step 6: Prepare your blender and pour half a cup of water into it.

Step 7: Put the heated rice into the blender.

Step 8: Blend at a slow to moderate speed until it reaches your desired consistency. (Progressively add a bit more water to soften the rice if needed)

Step 9: Strain the remaining chunks of rice from the rice-water solution into a container.

Step 10: Once strained, pour the rice-water solution into another clean pot and pour 1-2 tablespoons of vinegar then stir to remove odor and increase shelf life. (For a larger batch size, more vinegar may be needed)

Step 11: Pour 1-2 tablespoons of vegetable oil into the pot to further soften the rice-water solution. 

Step 12: Pour 1-2 tablespoons of baking soda into the pot to further improve the stickiness of the rice glue.

Step 13: After straining and mixing the vinegar, vegetable oil, and baking soda with the rice-water solution you can now pour it into your desired container then leave it for a few hours to cool.

Step 14: Use your rice glue and enjoy!

CHAPTER IV

RESULT AND DISCUSSION

To test the effectiveness and practical usage of the rice-based adhesive, the researchers decided it would be best to apply it on a few pieces of paper with different surfaces (Yellow Pad, Magazine, ChipBoard, etc.). This is to test its effectiveness on materials with various textures and thicknesses.

Three tests are performed to determine adhesive strength: the tensile strength test by pulling the glued objects apart, the shear strength test by sliding the objects apart, and the peel strength test by peeling one of the objects away from the other. These tests assess an adhesive's stickiness to specific objects and their effectiveness (BDK, 2022). After conducting the tests, the results showed that paper with a rougher texture is harder to pull, slide, and peel apart than paper with a smoother texture while paper that is thinner is easier to pull, slide, and peel apart than paper that is thicker.

For the adhesive’s sustainability, the researchers did two experiments. For the first attempt (left), the glue was stored inside a fridge for days while the second attempt (right) was stored outside a fridge. This is to simulate and determine its lifespan under two different environmental conditions and as shown in the pictures above, the refrigerated glue solidified while the unrefrigerated glue remained as a liquid, however,

To compare the researchers’ rice glue with any commercial glue like Elmer’s, the tensile strength test, shear strength test, and peeling test are conducted. The three types of glues, the refrigerated & unrefrigerated glue the researchers made and Elmer’s glue, are applied in between two pieces of paper separately and are left to cure for a few minutes before conducting the tests.

After conducting the tests, the results showed that the paper with refrigerated glue was indeed hard to pull and slide apart but was easier to peel, the paper with unrefrigerated glue was harder to pull, slide, and peel apart than the refrigerated glue, however, the paper with Elmer’s glue is the hardest to pull, slide, and peel apart. This shows that while commercial glue is better in general, rice glue remains a viable alternative to commercial glue when doing paper-related arts and crafts.

In conclusion, rice glue, although performs worse than any commercial glue, is effective on paper materials, is practical when doing paper-based activities, and is sustainable for a long period when stored properly in a sealed container. This not only makes it a viable eco-friendly alternative but also a viable alternative for commercial glue that students can use during their paper-based activities.
